Description

In M0THER you play as an AI gained sentience, looking through the lenses of security cameras. Your goal is to guide packs of cute but oh so stupid creatures through the deadly maze of the testing facility. You accomplish this by manipulating the facility and modifying the creatures giving them certain abilities.

My contribution

My main focus during this project was the various traps and interactables spread throughout the facility. And making the tools to set up and connect traps and interactables in an intuitive way in the editor.

I wanted to build the systems to be modular and versatile, so the designers could use them to create unique puzzles and interactions by just tweaking the values.

Basic trap functionality

The basic trap functionality is playing animations depending on different input. It can be activated or deactivated, usually meaning whether it can harm or block the creatures. For example opening or closing a door.
A trap can also be “triggered”, usually meaning a poor creature is about to die. For example a crusher smashing whatever is underneath.

Thanks to the modular nature of GDScript, every function can be overridden. So if for example a trap needs some special functionality when triggered, it can easily be implemented by overriding the function.

All traps also have built-in functionality for being interacted with directly by M0THER (Interaction functionality developed by Linus), toggling the activated state. This can of course be toggled off with a boolean.

Trap signals

The trap signals, using Godot’s awesome signal system, are what makes the trap system so versatile. Whenever a trap is triggered or the activation is toggled, it sends out a corresponding signal. That signal can be connected to the activation or trigger function of another trap, allowing designers to chain together trap events.
If for example a creature walks on a button, a door can open. Or if the player opens a door, a shredder can open its trapdoors.

It was however really annoying to set up. The designers had to open the signal menu, find the correct signal, and connect it to the right function on the target. And it was sometimes difficult to know exactly which function they were supposed to use on the different traps.

So I created some editor helper functionality letting them connect traps using drop-down menus with custom names for functions on certain traps, and connecting with just a few clicks. Connections show up on an easier to read list, where they can also be removed.

Notable traps

I will only talk about a select few of the traps I implemented. The ones that were more challenging or interesting, and how I overcame the challenges.

Wind vfx changing as the fans are blocked

The fan trap

The difficult part with the fan trap was making them blockable by obstacles, like closing a door for example.
I solved it by monitoring the collider for the fan's area of effect. If something - like a door - enters or exits the area, it waits until any animation is finished. Then it does a line trace to its maximum distance to see how far the area of effect stretches after this change.

This ended up working really well. It also gave me the data needed to adjust the wind vfx without doing any additional checks.

The bounce pads

The basic functionality of the bounce pad; to launch a creature in the aimed direction, was no problem to implement.
However, creatures with the small modifier are launched at such speeds that they should be killed if impacting a roof or wall. And this proved to be a really annoying to implement.
They had to be monitored, and if they collided with something with velocity in the right direction, they would turn into a pancake. But if they reached ground unharmed, they had to no longer be monitored.

It was also really annoying trying to aim the pancake model in the right direction according to the collision normal.

The first version of the bounce pad

Transport tube sucking in creatures

The transport tubes

Oh boy. These were a headache and a half.
The problem was not so much sucking in the creatures and spitting them out again, more so making it look good. I wanted the creatures to turn in the direction they were moving, and do it smoothly. And I wanted the tubes to be built up by multiple segments, which were rotated in a whole bunch of different ways.
I made the creatures do flips, spin around and turn 2D before I finally got them like I wanted.

In the end the tube segments didn’t really turn out that well, but after manually tweaking them across all levels they were good enough.

Other contributions

I did lots of stuff during this project, much of which I no longer even remember. But some I do.

One thing I made was the way the creatures tilt when falling or flying upward thanks to a bounce pad. I did it by using their velocity in y-axis to calculate how fast they should tilt and in what direction.
The end result turned out really nice and gave them a lot more life.

Another thing I made was a script for changing import settings in a large amount of files and reimporting them, per request of an artist. It took me several hours to research and write the script, the time it would have taken to do it manually. But with the tool we could do similar things with ease if we needed to again, which we actually did.
